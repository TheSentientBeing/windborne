SpaceX "Chopsticks"

The revolutionary booster recovery system that's redefining rocket reusability

In 2024, SpaceX successfully demonstrated a first-of-its-kind technology to catch and recover the Super Heavy booster using mechanical arms mounted on the launch tower.

Key Mechanical Components

Launch Tower ("Mechazilla")

The massive structure that houses the catching mechanism, designed to support the weight of the returning booster and provide a stable platform for the chopsticks.

"Chopsticks" (Catching Arms)

Two robotic arms mounted on the launch tower that extend and retract to catch the descending booster. They're designed with padding to minimize damage during the catch.

Super Heavy Booster

The first stage of the Starship rocket, propelled by 33 Raptor engines and designed to return to the launch site after separating from the Starship spacecraft.

Raptor Engines

Used by the booster to perform maneuvers during its descent, including boost-back burns and precision positioning for the catch.

Grid Fins

Used by the booster for aerodynamic control during its descent, these distinctive lattice-like structures help steer the rocket back to the launch site.

Precision Control Systems

Advanced guidance and control systems that coordinate the movements of both the booster and the chopsticks arms to achieve the precisely timed catch.

The Recovery Process

1. Boost and Separation

The Starship launches from the pad, powered by the Super Heavy booster. After reaching approximately 43 miles in altitude, the booster separates from the Starship spacecraft.

2. Boost-back Burn

The booster flips around and fires some of its Raptor engines to change its trajectory and head back towards the launch site.

3. Descent and Re-entry

The booster descends towards the launch site, using grid fins for control and experiencing atmospheric heating.

4. Hover and Alignment

As the booster approaches the launch tower, it slows to a near hover and performs a horizontal slide maneuver to align itself with the tower and the chopsticks.

5. "Chopsticks" Catch

The launch tower's mechanical arms ("chopsticks") extend and close in to catch the booster as it hovers above the launch pad. The arms are designed to grip the booster securely.

6. Securing and Reengagement

Once caught, the booster is securely held by the tower arms. The launch pad's systems are reengaged to prepare for inspection and refurbishment.

7. Refurbishment and Reuse

Being held by the tower, the booster is in position for inspections, maintenance, refueling, and preparation for its next flight.
